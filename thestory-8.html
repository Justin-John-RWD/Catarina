Government Intervenes in Property Dispute

On September 30, 2008, after a delay of over five months, the District Judge of Masaya formally acknowledged a request for third-party intervention by the Masaya delegate of the Procuraduría General de la República (PGR), representing the government.

This request sought to intervene in defense of supposed third-party interests regarding the property dispute involving Mr. Justin Dobson. The PGR’s motion cited the government’s issuance of the original Agrarian Reform Title as a basis for its continued interest in the case.

However, Mr. Dobson’s legal team argued that:

  • The government ceded its rights to the property when it issued the Agrarian Reform Title in 1983, a title that was later ratified in 2007. As such, the government no longer has any legal interest in the matter.
  • The intervention request was untimely, as the trial had concluded, and a final sentence had been issued in March 2008. The government had ample opportunity to address the matter earlier.
  • Allowing intervention at this stage would violate Mr. Dobson’s constitutional rights and contradict the government’s obligation to uphold Agrarian Reform titles.

Despite these arguments, the motion to intervene caused significant delays in executing the court-ordered eviction. The situation raised concerns about potential political or financial influence affecting the legal process.

Legal Proceedings Continue

The court eventually reopened the case to hear arguments regarding the PGR’s right to intervene. Mr. Dobson’s attorneys submitted a brief on October 3, 2008, reiterating their objections to the intervention, while the PGR repeated its claims without presenting new evidence.

Mr. Dobson expressed confidence in the judiciary, stating, “I am confident that the judge will rule according to the law and deny the PGR’s motion, allowing the execution of the sentence to proceed without further delay.”
